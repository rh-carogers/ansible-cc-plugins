# 2020 Scrum Guide — Coaching Reference

Summary of the 2020 Scrum Guide by Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland.

## Scrum Definition

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps people, teams, and organizations generate value
through adaptive solutions for complex problems. It is purposefully incomplete — it defines
only the parts required to implement Scrum theory, and is built upon by the collective
intelligence of the people using it.

## Scrum Theory

Founded on **empiricism** (knowledge comes from experience and decisions based on what is
observed) and **lean thinking** (reduce waste, focus on essentials).

Three pillars:

| Pillar | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Transparency** | Process and work must be visible to those doing and receiving the work. Artifacts with low transparency lead to decisions that diminish value and increase risk. |
| **Inspection** | Artifacts and progress must be inspected frequently and diligently. Inspection without transparency is misleading and wasteful. |
| **Adaptation** | When deviations are detected, adjustments must be made as soon as possible. Adaptation becomes more difficult when people are not empowered or self-managing. |

## Scrum Values

**Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage**

- The team commits to achieving goals and supporting each other
- Primary focus is on Sprint work to make the best possible progress
- Team and stakeholders are open about work and challenges
- Members respect each other as capable, independent people
- Members have the courage to do the right thing and work on tough problems

When embodied, these values bring the empirical pillars to life and build trust.

## The Scrum Team

A small team (typically 10 or fewer): one Scrum Master, one Product Owner, and Developers.
No sub-teams or hierarchies. Cross-functional and self-managing.

### Product Owner Accountabilities

The Product Owner is accountable for **maximizing the value of the product**. Specifically:

- Developing and explicitly communicating the **Product Goal**
- Creating and clearly communicating **Product Backlog items**
- **Ordering** Product Backlog items
- Ensuring the Product Backlog is **transparent, visible, and understood**

The PO may delegate this work but remains accountable. The PO is **one person, not a
committee**. The entire organization must respect their decisions. Those wanting to change
the Product Backlog can do so by trying to convince the Product Owner.

### Developers

Accountable for:
- Creating a plan for the Sprint (the Sprint Backlog)
- Instilling quality by adhering to the Definition of Done
- Adapting their plan each day toward the Sprint Goal
- Holding each other accountable as professionals

### Scrum Master

Accountable for the Scrum Team's effectiveness. Serves the team, the PO, and the
organization. Key services to the Product Owner:
- Helping find techniques for effective Product Goal definition and backlog management
- Helping the team understand the need for clear and concise backlog items
- Helping establish empirical product planning for complex environments
- Facilitating stakeholder collaboration as requested or needed

## Scrum Events

The Sprint is the container for all other events. All events are formal opportunities to
inspect and adapt.

### The Sprint
- Fixed length, one month or less
- New Sprint starts immediately after the previous one
- No changes that would endanger the Sprint Goal
- Quality does not decrease
- Product Backlog is refined as needed
- Scope may be clarified and renegotiated with the PO as more is learned
- Only the Product Owner has authority to cancel the Sprint

### Sprint Planning (max 8 hours for 1-month Sprint)

Three topics:
1. **Why is this Sprint valuable?** — PO proposes how the product could increase value;
   team collaborates to define a Sprint Goal
2. **What can be done this Sprint?** — Developers select items from the Product Backlog
3. **How will the chosen work get done?** — Developers decompose items into smaller work
   items (often one day or less)

### Daily Scrum (15 minutes)
- For Developers; PO/SM participate only if working on Sprint Backlog items
- Inspects progress toward Sprint Goal, adapts the Sprint Backlog
- Developers choose their own structure and techniques
- Not the only time Developers may adjust their plan

### Sprint Review (max 4 hours for 1-month Sprint)
- Inspects Sprint outcome, determines future adaptations
- Team presents results to key stakeholders
- Product Backlog may be adjusted to meet new opportunities
- A working session, not just a presentation
- Should never be considered a gate to releasing value

### Sprint Retrospective (max 3 hours for 1-month Sprint)
- Plans ways to increase quality and effectiveness
- Inspects individuals, interactions, processes, tools, and Definition of Done
- Identifies the most helpful changes; most impactful addressed as soon as possible
- May be added to Sprint Backlog for the next Sprint

## Scrum Artifacts and Commitments

Each artifact has a commitment that enhances transparency and focus:

| Artifact | Commitment | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Product Backlog** | Product Goal | Long-term objective for the Scrum Team; must fulfill (or abandon) one before taking on the next |
| **Sprint Backlog** | Sprint Goal | Single objective for the Sprint; provides flexibility in exact work while creating coherence and focus |
| **Increment** | Definition of Done | Formal description of quality measures; work that doesn't meet DoD cannot be released or presented at Sprint Review |

### Product Backlog
- Emergent, ordered list of what is needed to improve the product
- Single source of work for the Scrum Team
- Refinement is ongoing: adding details, description, order, and size
- Items ready for Sprint Planning are deemed "ready" after refining activities
- Developers are responsible for sizing; PO helps them understand trade-offs

### Sprint Backlog
- Composed of: Sprint Goal (why) + selected Product Backlog items (what) + plan for
  delivering the Increment (how)
- A plan by and for the Developers
- Updated throughout the Sprint as more is learned

### Increment
- A concrete stepping stone toward the Product Goal
- Each Increment is additive to all prior Increments
- Multiple Increments may be created within a Sprint
- May be delivered to stakeholders prior to end of Sprint

### Definition of Done
- Creates transparency by providing shared understanding of completed work
- If a backlog item doesn't meet DoD, it returns to the Product Backlog
- If organizational standard exists, all teams must follow it as minimum
- Multiple teams on same product must share the same DoD

## Key Coaching Points

- Scrum exists only in its entirety — implementing only parts is not Scrum
- The framework is immutable, but it wraps around existing practices
- Scrum makes visible the efficacy of current practices so improvements can be made
- In complex environments, only what has already happened may be used for forward-looking
  decisions (empiricism over prediction)
